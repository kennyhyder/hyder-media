1998 Agreed Judgment — Key Terms

Case: No. 98-1177-3, McLennan County, TX — 74th Judicial District, signed April 23, 1998.

Parties: Dunnam & Dunnam LLP (Plaintiffs) v. The Dunham Law Firm, Attorneys at Law, P.C., Paul J. Dunham, Jeffrey R. Casey, Tony Faulkner, and Bobby Hawkins (Defendants).

Core restriction: Defendants permanently enjoined from using, directly or indirectly, in the following counties:

Bell McLennan Coryell Hill Bosque Johnson Brazos Hamilton Somervell Limestone Freestone Robertson Falls Madison Navarro Leon

Prohibited names: "Dunham, Attorney at Law," "Dunhams, Attorney at Law," "Dunham Law Firm," "Dunham Firm," "Dunhams," "Dunham and Associates," and any variation using "Dunham" or "Dunnam" with the word "The" or "the," or with abbreviations (Inc., P.C., LLP), or names with the spelling "Dunnam" rather than "Dunham," or "Dunham" or "Dunnam" with a different given or surname.

Advertising restriction: Defendants shall cancel all advertising or telephone listings using the specified names in the specified area, and shall not hereafter place any advertising or solicitation emphasizing "Dunham" or "Dunnam" in the specified area.

Key limitation: The judgment restricts the use of specific names in specific counties. It does not restrict the defendants from practicing law or advertising their services in those counties under non-prohibited names (e.g., "Dunham & Jones").

I. Summary of Opinions

Report pp. 1

Brown's Claims

Brown claims that defendants' domains continue to show "ongoing exposure in restricted counties" and "continued algorithmic association with the Dunnam & Dunnam brand." She asserts the "net consumer-facing and algorithmic effect has not materially retreated" since August 2025, and that visibility has merely "shifted between DunhamLaw.com and DunhamJones.com" while the "aggregate footprint remains substantially similar."

Speculative Overreach

Key Assertions to Rebut

1. "Ongoing exposure in restricted counties" — implies active targeting, but Brown admits she cannot verify geo-targeting settings without internal data.
2. "Continued algorithmic association with the Dunnam & Dunnam brand" — conflates organic search engine behavior (which defendants don't control) with intentional conduct.
3. "Aggregate footprint" theory — treats two separate legal entities/domains as a single actor to inflate the appearance of non-compliance.
4. "Compliance assertions cannot be verified without defendants' internal documentation" — frames the absence of evidence as evidence of non-compliance.

Brown's Claims

Burden Shifting

Key Assertions to Rebut

1. Brown repeatedly "refrains from assuming compliance" — effectively presumes guilt and shifts the burden of proof to the defendants.
2. Scope creep: the 1998 judgment predates modern search engines and addresses naming and advertising, not SEO practices, algorithmic behavior, or domain strategies.

IV. Findings A — DunhamLaw.com

Report pp. 3–4

Brown's Claims

A1 — Domain Use: DunhamLaw.com is active, "Dunham" appears in URLs/titles/headers/meta content, and the site "marketed services tied to restricted counties" with "Waco-related phrasing."

A2 — Brand-Adjacent Visibility: DunhamLaw.com ranked for "dunnam & dunnam," "dunnam and dunnam," and "dunnam and dunnam Waco" in third-party organic data. She concedes paid advertising "cannot be verified" without account exports.

A3 — Geographic Targeting: Site promoted services in McLennan County (Waco) and other restricted counties with "county mentions and Waco-specific phrasing at scale."

A4 — Keyword Counts (Aug 2025):

  • "Waco criminal attorney" — ~450 mentions
  • "Bell County" — ~195
  • "Brazos County" — ~70
  • "Coryell County" — ~65
  • "Hill County" — ~55
  • "Bosque County" — ~60
  • "Johnson County" — ~50
  • Other restricted counties — ~20–45 each
Inflated Counts No Methodology for Counts Conflates Name with Prohibited Name

Key Assertions to Rebut

1. "Dunham" is the defendants' actual legal name — the 1998 judgment prohibits specific name formulations like "Dunham Law Firm" in specific counties, not the use of the surname "Dunham" generally. "DunhamLaw.com" vs. "Dunham Law Firm" is a critical distinction.
2. Ranking for "dunnam & dunnam" queries is organic search engine behavior — defendants have no control over Google's algorithm choosing to surface DunhamLaw.com for "Dunnam" queries due to name similarity.
3. Keyword counts (450 mentions of "Waco criminal attorney") are unverified — no crawl methodology, date, dedup method, or distinction between unique pages vs. templated/paginated content.
4. County mentions may reflect legitimate practice areas — the judgment restricts the use of certain names in those counties, not the practice of law or all advertising there.
5. Brown concedes she cannot verify paid search targeting but still implies violation through "brand-adjacent visibility" language.

V. Findings B — DunhamJones.com

Report pp. 5

Brown's Claims

B1 — Domain Timeline: DunhamJones.com registered June 17, 2011; first Wayback capture February 20, 2023. Brown suggests "restricted-county content may now be hosted on DunhamJones.com rather than DunhamLaw.com."

B2 — Content Migration: Third-party data shows DunhamJones.com ranks for "Waco-specific and other restricted-county criminal defense queries." She calls this "content migration or re-optimization rather than removal."

B3 — Paid Search: No paid keyword activity detected for DunhamJones.com, but Brown argues this "does not establish compliance" because campaigns "can be paused/reactivated."

Speculative "Aggregate Footprint" Theory

Key Assertions to Rebut

1. "Dunham & Jones" is NOT one of the prohibited names in the 1998 Agreed Judgment. The judgment lists specific names; "Dunham & Jones" is not among them.
2. Brown's "aggregate footprint" theory has no legal basis — she is treating two distinct domains and entities as interchangeable to manufacture a violation.
3. Ranking for "Waco criminal defense" queries is not itself a violation — the judgment restricts certain names, not the right to practice or advertise under non-prohibited names.
4. Finding no paid activity but arguing it "doesn't establish compliance" is unfalsifiable reasoning — no evidence could ever satisfy this standard.

VII. Intent, Ability to Comply, and Post-Notice Conduct

Report pp. 7

Brown's Claims

A — Intent: Because defendants manage their sites, they have "technical capacity" to comply, and any non-compliance "is not attributable to technical impossibility" but rather "selective implementation choices."

B — Post-Notice: Defendants continued modifying their digital presence after being on notice, but "core elements... have not been conclusively cured." She calls this "relevant evidence because it shows compliance steps were feasible but not completed."

C — Algorithmic/AI Confusion: A chatbot on defendants' website "surfaced incorrect or conflicting contact information associated with Dunnam & Dunnam." Brown admits this "was not consistently reproducible" and is "not relied upon as a standalone finding."

Imputes Intent Legal Conclusions Non-Reproducible AI Anecdote

Key Assertions to Rebut

1. Brown is offering legal conclusions about intent and willfulness — this exceeds the scope of a technical expert and invades the province of the fact-finder.
2. "Selective implementation choices" implies intentional violation without any supporting evidence.
3. The chatbot anecdote is self-admittedly non-reproducible and irrelevant — defendants don't control third-party AI chatbot behavior.
4. "Algorithmic ambiguity" from overlapping names is an inherent consequence of two firms with similar surnames — not evidence of misconduct.

VIII. Technical Simplicity of Compliance

Report pp. 8

Brown's Claims

Brown asserts that compliance is "technically straightforward and comparatively inexpensive," listing: disabling DNS for "prohibited domains," deactivating/redirecting those domains, removing prohibited references from content/metadata/structured data, Search Console URL inspection and re-index requests, and negative keyword implementation with geo-exclusions in paid channels.

Assumes Violations Exist

Key Assertions to Rebut

1. This section presumes non-compliance and then argues compliance would be easy — but never establishes what specific violations need remediation.
2. "Disabling DNS for prohibited domains" — which domains does she consider "prohibited"? DunhamLaw.com uses the defendants' legal surname.
3. Negative keyword implementation for "protected marks" — "Dunham" is not a protected mark; it is the defendants' actual name. The judgment restricts specific name formulations, not the surname itself.
4. Brown's "compliance steps" would effectively require defendants to suppress their own legal name from search engines — going far beyond what the 1998 judgment orders.

XIII. Corrective Advertising

Report pp. 13–14

Brown's Claims

Brown proposes multi-year corrective advertising to "unwind historical associations," arguing that search engine associations "do not self-correct simply because conduct stops." She proposes annual costs of:

  • Paid search corrective ads: $25,000–$50,000/year
  • SEO corrective content & signal repair: $20,000–$35,000/year
  • Directory/platform remediation: $8,000–$15,000/year
  • Ongoing monitoring & enforcement: $12,000–$20,000/year
  • Total: $65,000–$120,000/year for multiple years
No Basis for Estimates Grossly Inflated Assumes Ongoing Violation

Key Assertions to Rebut

1. No methodology, market data, or comparable cases cited to support the dollar figures. For a regional Waco market, these numbers are vastly inflated.
2. Brown charges $125/hour but proposes $12,000–$20,000/year for "monitoring" — this appears designed to create an ongoing revenue stream for her own firm.
3. "Associations do not self-correct" is misleading — search engine results change constantly, and historical SEO signals decay naturally over weeks/months, not the "multi-year" period she asserts.
4. The entire corrective advertising section presumes proven violations and proven damages — neither has been established.
5. "Paid search corrective ads" of $25K–$50K/year in a regional market like Waco is orders of magnitude beyond what would be needed for any legitimate clarification campaign.
