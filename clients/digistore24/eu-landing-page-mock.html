EU VAT · OSS · IOSS

Do US sellers have to charge EU VAT on digital products?

Yes — from your very first sale. Unlike US sales tax, there is no minimum threshold for non-EU businesses selling digital products or services to EU consumers. The moment a customer in Berlin buys your $49 course, you technically owe German VAT on that sale.

  • VAT rates range from 17% to 27% depending on the customer's country — and you must charge the rate where your customer lives, not where you're based.
  • You'd need to register — either in individual member states or through the EU's non-Union One Stop Shop (OSS) scheme, which lets you file one quarterly return covering all 27 countries. Physical goods imported under €150 use the separate Import One Stop Shop (IOSS).
  • Every sale needs a VAT-compliant invoice, and B2B sales to VAT-registered European businesses are handled differently (reverse charge) than B2C sales.
  • Getting it wrong isn't theoretical — EU tax authorities can and do pursue foreign sellers, and marketplaces now report seller revenue directly to them (see DAC7 below).

Taxes · Filing · DAC7

Sales tax vs. VAT — and what a US seller actually files

US sales tax is charged once, at the final sale, and only where you have nexus. VAT is different: it's charged on every transaction in the chain, at much higher rates (17–27% vs. the 6–10% US sellers are used to), and it's owed to a foreign tax authority in a foreign filing system.

  • Selling direct means foreign tax exposure: VAT returns (quarterly under OSS), potential fiscal representatives, and currency conversion on every remittance.
  • DAC7 requires digital platforms to report sellers' revenues to EU tax authorities annually — EU tax offices increasingly see cross-border digital income by default.
  • Selling digital products into the EU generally does not create an EU "permanent establishment" for US income-tax purposes on its own — but direct-selling setups (local entities, warehouses, dependent agents) can change that. Most US sellers want to stay far away from that line.

Payments · Currencies · PSD2

What payment methods do European customers actually use?

Here's what surprises most US sellers: in much of Europe, credit cards are not the default. If your checkout only takes Visa and Mastercard in USD, you're silently losing a large share of European buyers at the last step.

Netherlands

iDEAL

The dominant Dutch payment method — direct bank transfer used for the majority of online purchases in the Netherlands.

Germany · Austria · Nordics

Klarna / Sofort

Pay-later and direct bank transfer. German buyers in particular expect invoice and installment options at checkout.

Belgium

Bancontact

Belgium's standard online payment scheme — the expected option for Belgian consumers.

Eurozone-wide

SEPA Direct Debit

Bank-to-bank payments across the euro area — widely used for subscriptions and installment plans.

EU-wide requirement

PSD2 / 3-D Secure

European law requires Strong Customer Authentication on most online card payments. Checkouts without proper 3DS flows see failed transactions.

Everywhere

Local currency & language

Buyers convert better when they pay in euros (or their home currency) with a checkout in their own language.

GDPR · Privacy · Cookies

Does GDPR apply to a US business selling to Europe?

Yes. GDPR applies to any business — anywhere in the world — that offers goods or services to people in the EU or monitors their behavior. Selling to European customers puts you in scope, regardless of where your LLC is registered.

  • Consent rules: valid cookie consent, opt-in (not pre-checked) marketing consent, and the right for users to access or delete their data.
  • Documentation: a GDPR-grade privacy policy, data processing agreements with your vendors, and records of processing activities.
  • Enforcement is real: fines can reach 4% of global revenue, and EU regulators have pursued non-EU companies.
  • Handling European payment data yourself multiplies your exposure — cardholder data plus personal data across 27 jurisdictions.

Consumer Law · Refunds · Invoicing

The 14-day rule: EU consumer rights on digital purchases

EU consumer law gives buyers a 14-day right of withdrawal on online purchases — a legally mandated cooling-off period, not a courtesy refund policy. For digital products there's a critical nuance:

  • The withdrawal right for digital content can be waived — but only if the buyer expressly consents to immediate delivery and acknowledges losing the withdrawal right, captured the right way at checkout, before delivery.
  • Your terms, refund policy, and pre-contract information must meet EU distance-selling disclosure requirements — US-style "all sales final" language is unenforceable against EU consumers.
  • EU customers (and their accountants) expect compliant invoices — with VAT identification and country-correct formatting.
  • The EU's geo-blocking rules also restrict unjustified discrimination between EU customers based on their country.

Fulfillment · 3PL · Shipping

What about physical products — shipping, customs, and 3PL?

Selling digital products to Europe requires no logistics at all — delivery is instant and borderless, which is exactly why digital is the fastest way to go international. If your funnel includes physical components (books, supplements, merch), three things matter:

  • EORI number: importing commercial goods into the EU requires an Economic Operators Registration and Identification number for customs.
  • Customs, duties & import VAT: shipments face import VAT (IOSS simplifies orders under €150) and possible duties — surprise fees at the door kill repeat purchases.
  • European 3PL: at volume, US sellers typically move inventory to a European fulfillment center (Germany and the Netherlands are the common hubs) so delivery takes days, not weeks, and customs is handled once per bulk shipment instead of per order.

Selling in Europe: frequently asked questions

Do I need to register for EU VAT if I sell through Digistore24?
No. Digistore24 operates as a reseller (merchant of record), which makes Digistore24 the seller in each customer transaction. Digistore24 calculates, collects, and remits VAT across the EU — you never register, file, or remit European VAT for sales made through the platform.
Do US companies have to charge EU VAT on digital products?
If you sell directly to EU consumers — yes, from the first sale. There is no minimum threshold for non-EU sellers of digital products. Selling through a merchant of record moves that obligation to the platform instead.
What's the difference between OSS and IOSS?
The One Stop Shop (OSS) is the EU's single quarterly VAT filing scheme covering digital products and services sold across all member states. The Import One Stop Shop (IOSS) is the equivalent for physical goods imported into the EU in shipments valued under €150. Both exist so sellers don't have to register in every individual country — and neither is needed at all when a merchant of record is the seller.
Does GDPR apply to my US business?
Yes — GDPR applies to any business offering goods or services to people in the EU, regardless of where the business is located. Selling through Digistore24 keeps customer transaction data on the platform's GDPR-compliant infrastructure, which significantly reduces (but doesn't eliminate) your own compliance footprint — your website and marketing lists remain your responsibility.
What payment methods do European customers expect?
It varies by country: iDEAL in the Netherlands, Klarna/Sofort in Germany and Austria, Bancontact in Belgium, SEPA Direct Debit across the eurozone, plus cards with PSD2-compliant 3-D Secure authentication everywhere. The Digistore24 checkout presents each customer with the local methods, currency, and language they expect.
Can EU customers demand refunds on digital products?
EU law provides a 14-day right of withdrawal on online purchases. For digital content, that right can be waived when the buyer expressly consents to immediate delivery and acknowledges the waiver at checkout — a flow that's built into the Digistore24 order process.
What is DAC7 and does it affect me?
DAC7 is an EU directive requiring digital platforms to report sellers' revenues to European tax authorities annually. It's handled at the platform level — one more reporting regime that isn't your problem when you sell through a compliant platform rather than running your own EU storefront.
Do I need an EORI number or a European 3PL?
Only for physical goods. Digital products need no customs, shipping, or logistics of any kind. If you sell physical products into the EU at volume, you'll want an EORI number for customs and, eventually, a European fulfillment partner — Germany and the Netherlands are the common hubs.
